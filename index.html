The one idea to take away

An optimal trajectory isn't a guess. It has a structure you can prove.

For a system that has to reach a target as fast as possible, the best control is not smooth and gradual. It is decisive: push hard, then brake hard, switching at exactly the right instant. That switch is dictated by a hidden quantity called the costate. My research computes where those switches happen — and how the whole strategy changes as the starting point changes.

01 / STATE

Where the system is

Position and velocity. In the demo, how far the car is from its target and how fast it's going.

02 / CONTROL

What you can push

Accelerate or brake, bounded. The optimal choice is almost always all-the-way one direction, then the other — this is "bang-bang" control.

03 / THE MAP

Every start, pre-solved

Split the space of starting conditions into regions. Each region has one optimal strategy. That partition is the explicit solution.

In discrete time, this same problem fragments into many more regions — one per active-set combination at each step. The continuous-time formulation keeps it to a handful, because each region is one arc sequence. That structural reduction is the point of the work.
